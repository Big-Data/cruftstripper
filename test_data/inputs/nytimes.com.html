Leaving the Light On for Playwrights

Tony Cenicola/The New York Times, left; Darren Higgins for The New York Times, center; Nicole Bengiveno/The New York Times

Molly Smith, center, the artistic director of the Arena Stage in Washington, has overseen a three-year expansion. Melanie Joseph, left, is having a work produced at the theater; Katori Hall, right, has used the town house it makes available to playwrights.

By PATRICK HEALY
Published: January 14, 2011
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Single Page
  • Reprints

WASHINGTON

WHEN Molly Smith began envisioning a third theater space here for Arena Stage, as part of the recent $135 million transformation that she led as artistic director, she grappled with how to distill her ideas into a clear form. Then one day she found herself staring at her hands. “I cupped them together, and there it was, that was our theater,” Ms. Smith recalled. “When you look inside two cupped hands, you have a cradle. That’s what I wanted: a small, enveloping cradle where we could nurture and stage newly birthed American plays.”

Cradle is not a word typically associated with playwriting. Many writers do not look to theaters for care and feeding, let alone a security blanket. What you often hear about instead is crummy pay, no health insurance, not enough time for rehearsals, and enormous frustrations in persuading major regional houses like Arena Stage to mount the second or third production of a new play, given how many artistic directors prize world premieres. And those artistic directors are no pollyannas. Staying afloat is tough business, they say, with theaters like Intiman in Seattle, the Pasadena Playhouse and others that have endured financial struggles in this era of diminished donations. Producing largely unknown plays is usually a riskier bet at the box office than a revival of a classic. (Paging Willy Loman!)

So it is an act of some theatrical audacity, really, that Arena Stage has christened its new 200-seat space as the Arlene and Robert Kogod Cradle (named for benefactors who donated $7.5 million) and endowed it with the mission “of developing new American plays and cradling artistic risk.” But the Cradle is itself part of an audacious act: Arena Stage’s effort to brand itself as a national center for producing, presenting, developing and studying American theater. At a time of great competition for grant and foundation money among nonprofit theaters Arena is seeking to stand out among the 75 or so major regional theaters by building on its 60-year-old roots in Washington, stressing the word “American” in its messages to audience members and convening events to focus on the craft of playwriting and careers of playwrights.

“I think we throw away some of our best new American plays like tissue paper,” Ms. Smith said, standing on the stage of the sloping, oval-shaped Cradle during a recent tour, her voice bouncing clearly off of the dark-stained wood walls. “Our strongest writers keep falling away from the theater because they can’t make a living. And without writers we can’t have a theater. So we are responsible for them. We are responsible, I think, for turning our theaters into artistic homes.”

After reopening in October after an extensive three-year expansion — the theater complex is now formally called Arena Stage at the Mead Center for American Theater — Ms. Smith and her colleagues quickly began putting their branding plans into action. The inaugural production, a revival of “Oklahoma!” with a multiracial cast and directed by Ms. Smith, sought to celebrate American optimism, frontier spirit and the origins of a new state; the show, in Arena’s 683-seat Fichandler Stage, its main performance space, broke several box-office records and drew some interest from Broadway producers. (The show concluded its run in December, and no Broadway transfer is in the immediate offing.)

The Cradle opened meanwhile with a world premiere of “every tongue confess,” by the 33-year-old writer Marcus Gardley, a play about black life and church burnings in the South, with themes and characters that reflect Washington’s predominantly black population. (Arena’s third stage is the 514-seat Kreeger Theater, where Anna Deavere Smith’s one-woman show “Let Me Down Easy,” which has played in New York and elsewhere, is running through mid-February.)

On Wednesday Arena will mount a New Play Festival, a fairly modest endeavor that features full productions of works by Melanie Joseph and Claudia Rankine and by the Rude Mechs theater troupe as well as readings of works by, among others, Rajiv Joseph, whose “Gruesome Playground Injuries” and “Bengal Tiger in the Baghdad Zoo” are both opening in New York this year. And next week Arena’s American Voices New Play Institute will hold a four-day conference on the state of the playwriting field for new works.

That gathering will include the unveiling of an intriguing online project, the New Play Map, in which theaters around the country can log onto a database and enter production histories for new plays, details about the writers, and other information. This version will be text-heavy; future versions could become more visually arresting, perhaps with photography or even video of shows included on the site. The map is also an attempt to help writers who complain of feeling isolated in their towns or cities, by giving them a guide to theaters and artists that might be inclined to their work.

“If a writer, for instance, feels their work is in the spirit or vein of a writer like Lynn Nottage or Tracy Letts, they can look at the map to get a sharper sense of what theaters have a history of producing those writers,” said David Dower, Arena’s associate artistic director, who oversees new play development.
